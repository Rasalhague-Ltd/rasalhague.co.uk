The Problem

What actually happens when no one owns the issue

A small problem can become serious fast. A tenant reports a leak with a photo and a short message — the way most housing issues are still raised today. No one replies. The message moves up the thread and disappears from view.

By the next evening, water has reached the wiring and is running from the power sockets. Six people are still living in the house, still paying rent, and still waiting for help.

This is not an extreme example. It is a common failure pattern — and I know because I lived inside it.

This is how UK shared housing too often works: a 650,000-property sector where urgent problems can disappear into informal messages and tenants are left chasing silence.

Lived Experience

Different tenancies. The same failure pattern.

I saw the same issues repeat across four shared properties in Newcastle, as well as stays with friends in Manchester and London. Whether the property was agency-managed, privately owned, or part of a landlord portfolio, the operating pattern rarely changed.

  • 01Damp was blamed on tenants drying clothes indoors.
  • 02A leaking sewer flooded the backyard, yet the tenant was asked to arrange the repair.
  • 03Mould was blamed on a cat living in the home.
  • 04Faulty heating equipment left an old property dangerously overheated.
  • 05A tenant’s bedroom door was forced during an attempted break-in.
  • 06A heating system smelled like rotten eggs, leading to the fire service being called.
  • 07A broken fence was repeatedly patched with its own broken pieces.
  • 08Tenants were threatened with eviction, and belongings were removed from rooms without consent.
  • 09Severe mould and damp were covered with fresh paint or new carpet instead of being properly resolved.

The buildings were not the only problem. The operating model was. There was no reliable reporting process, no triage, no response timeline, no verified closure, and no consequence for delay.

The Shift

From maritime discipline to housing accountability

Ships don’t run on goodwill. Homes shouldn’t either.

Before building Rasalhague, I served in the merchant navy as a Navigating Officer on oil tankers. That experience shaped how I think about safety, responsibility, and operational discipline.

Ships stay safe because their systems are clear, auditable, and difficult to ignore. Hazards are classified. Response times are defined. Actions are recorded. Nothing important depends on memory, mood, or goodwill.

Rasalhague applies that discipline to UK shared housing: every issue is structured, assigned, timed, recorded, and closed with evidence.

What It Is

Not another admin tool. An operating layer for responsibility.

Rasalhague is the operational governance layer for UK shared housing. It is built on one simple rule: every issue needs an owner, a timeframe, a record, and a verified close.

It brings landlords and tenants into the same workflow. Tenants can report issues, see them prioritised, follow the response, and confirm completion without being reduced to names on a file.

Structured reports replace scattered messages. Urgency is assessed, response windows are set, and the works log remains permanent and uneditable.

No lost chat threads. No vague promises. No mould blamed on the cats.

How It Works

Three clear steps. One accountable record.

  • 01Report the issue. Tenants raise repairs, hazards, and maintenance concerns through structured reports instead of informal messages that can be missed.
  • 02Prioritise the response. Each issue is logged, ranked by urgency, and given a defined response timeframe. The process does not depend on mood, memory, or goodwill.
  • 03Resolve and verify completion. Closure requires evidence, and every action remains part of a permanent works log that cannot be quietly changed later.
Built On Evidence

Evidence-led. Standards-aligned. Designed for real-world housing.

Rasalhague is not built on opinion. Its case is supported by peer-reviewed academic research into tenant experience and landlord operations, drawn from two independent UK university studies.

The platform is designed around current UK housing health and safety standards and aligned with the direction of private rented sector reform.

In settings where responsibility is institutionalised — such as student accommodation or regulated landlord housing — outcomes improve because issues are captured, assigned, monitored, and evidenced.

Most shared housing has never had an equivalent operating standard. That is the gap Rasalhague is built to close.
